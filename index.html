The fellowship

The fellowship connects passionate, talented and experienced people to local municipalities throughout our country. These coders, designers, data scientists, and product managers give a year of service because they want to give back and help make cities a better place to live.

Featured Apps

textizen.com

Textizen

Citizen feedback is essential to the healthy functioning of any democracy, but in-person meetings are personnel-intensive, expensive, and often result in non-representative feedback. Our city partners on the Philadelphia City Planning Commission lamented that many residents couldn’t make it to the few meetings the agency could afford to hold, and that those who did often only represented vocal minorities.

In Philadelphia, 41% of residents don’t have access to broadband internet at home, but over 90% have access to mobile phones with text messaging. The CfA Fellows designed, built and successfully piloted Textizen to collect feedback via SMS, allowing an organization to create surveys and collect responses via text message so that traditionally disenfranchised groups can participate. Other cities have taken note, and the fellows are launching a start-up. They've received enthusiastic inquiries from more than 110 cities across the United States, are working with 15 right now, and have already re-deployed in Boston and Salt Lake City.

answers.honolulu.gov

Honolulu Answers

Early on a Saturday in July, more than 55 Honolulu community members came together to write content for the city’s new website – Honolulu Answers. Though the existing Honolulu.gov website has a wealth of great information, citizens found it difficult to navigate and challenging to find the specific information they were looking for. In live user testing with residents, CfA fellows found that people visiting Honolulu.gov weren’t there to browse; they were in search of an answer to a specific question.

From the ground up, Honolulu Answers was designed to be people-centric. Our team designed “Quick Answers” pages that addressed just one question, starting with the most frequent questions based on city Customer Service Department data and website analytics. Citizens who need more can branch off into more detailed “Guides.” Honolulu Answers is not only driven by citizen questions, it’s also driven by citizen- focused writing. Code for America and the City of Honolulu hosted a “civic write-a-thon” to write approachable, easy-to-understand answers.

blightstatus.com

BlightStatus

In New Orleans, countless organizations are working around the clock to improve local communities and reduce blight throughout the city. However, each group manages data about blighted properties individually, which results in duplicated efforts and inconsistent information.

Partnering with the city and the Redevelopment Authority, the fellows created BlightStatus to make it simple for residents to find out what’s going on with blighted properties in their community – no long waits on the telephone or visits to City Hall required.

BlightStatus pulls property information directly from the city, providing a single, comprehensive and authoritative view. The public can check out a property’s status bar, then scroll down to the detailed case history for a step-by-step description of what’s happened so far. Citizens can also subscribe to receive instant alerts the moment the status changes on properties that they care about.

A thriving ecosystem for civic innovation requires a healthy marketplace, engaged and active communities, and a support system for innovators inside governments.

That’s what we’re building with our newest programs.

Building Community as Capacity

In times of shrinking budgets and higher demands on government, it is essential to find new ways for local government to operate. Citizens are showing they can take on more responsibility and governments are seeing the community as capacity to help with things such as digging out fire hydrants in the snow, helping shape city plans and designs, and even rewriting government websites.

The Brigade provides a national platform to help these groups come together, and for others, the tools and resources to start a local chapter in their community. This growing network of civic leaders is working to redefine the notion of citizenship at the local level.

Building a Market for Civic Innovation

The past few decades have seen staggering advances in technology, but government has been left behind, saddled with outdated and inefficient software, while costs have exploded into the hundreds of billions. New technology and startups, however, are emerging to create a new marketplace for civic innovation -- one that not only saves governments time and money, but also helps them connect and engage with citizens in new ways.

The Code for America Accelerator recruits “civic startups” and turbo-charges them with a national platform to raise awareness, a wealth of business training and advice, and a broad network of potential investors and civic leaders.

Empowering Local Change Agents

Local governments are changing. Forward-thinking public servants across the country are leveraging technology to innovate the way our cities work — and it’s accelerated by the exchange of ideas, solutions, best practices, and even software code between like-minded leaders. Cities are now appointing Chief Data Officers and Chief Innovation Officers; they are redeploying each other’s apps; and they are working together to take on common policy challenges. This new network of civic leaders is showing that real change is possible from inside city hall.

The Code for America Peer Network is a professional learning network for local government innovators who share the common goal of taking innovation in their city to the next level. Through the Peer Network, Code for America connects local governments around the country to facilitate peer learning, collaborative problem-solving, and the spread of innovation best practices between cities.
